Many organizations practicing 'agile' are actually just running a different flavor of project management. They adopt the vocabulary of agile—relying heavily on 'S' words like Scrum, squads, and sprints—but their goals remain fixed on managing team outputs. They create systems designed to push features rather than build the capacity to adapt

There is nothing inherently bad about wanting to improve how teams get work done and their ability to meet delivery commitments, but the issue is when the focus on those goals limit what else the system can do.

Optimizing for the delivery of software is not the same as optimizing for organizational agility. Pursuing greater team throughput by installing practices rooted in command-and-control creates an environment that will reject practices focused on improving collaboration and team autonomy.

By over-optimizing for delivery, the organization installs practices and systems that make it less agile and more fragile.

## A Project Manager in Agile Clothing

"Agile delivery" inherits its orientation from traditional project management: Like a factory, the goal is to keep the work moving. Under this model, the Project Manager's title changes to "Agile Delivery Lead", but the responsibilities and management systems are still rooted in traditional top-down project management.

If the goal is to maximize output by having management design, install and control a standardized team operating model, your system will have to adopt values and practices that oppose the values and practices that enable agility.

The two goals are mutually exclusive:

- **Optimize for Throughput:** Focuses on moving through the backlog, and pushing work through teams.
- **Optimize for Agility:** Focuses on the system's to adapt and change. It prioritizes environment design, psychological safety, and autonomous work groups over standardized practices 

Just like with Product Owners and Scrum Masters, the role of an Agile Delivery Lead harms teams when it is implemented as a rigid, individual job description rather than a shared group responsibility.

My ideal version of a Product Owner is a team enabler—someone who helps the team make better product choices by deeply understanding customers and their needs. Similarly, a Scrum Master should also be a team enabler, helping the group improve its engineering and collaboration practices

It's a common pattern organizations fall back on whenever a priority becomes important. They assign one person to be responsible for product, one for the team, one for architecture, one for code quality, and one for design. Agile Delivery Leads often find themselves above the rest of the team, propagating the pattern of managing teams using authority and control while using the language of agile.

The idea of cross-functional teams is often mistaken as putting people with different skills on the same team. What we rarely see is the cultivation of teams composed of multi-skilled, multi-disciplinary _individuals_. The more an organization relies on specialized, individual job assignments to mitigate risks, the less flexibility it has.

| Dimension | Delivery-Focused Approach | Agility-Focused Approach |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Goal** | Delivery of high-value outputs. | Develop flexibility and the ability to pivot easily. |
| **Team Model** | Standardized structures with highly specialized individual roles. | Flexible, autonomous groups composed of multi-skilled, adaptive individuals. |
| **Management Style** | Agile Delivery Leads has authority and is responsible for leading agile projects. | Environment designed to enable team autonomy and safety. |
| **Learning Model** | **Single-Loop:** Fixing immediate delivery failures to go faster. | **Double-Loop:** Questions assumptions and ways of working. |
| **Systemic Risk** | **Calcification:** The delivery machinery becomes too rigid to change in ways that doesn't directly support delivery. | **Short-Term Friction:** Slower initial setup as capabilities are built and existing structures are disrupted. |
| **Vulnerability** | Fragile to market or environment shifts. | Vulnerable to leadership demanding immediate, legible metrics. |

## Optimizing for Delivery Limits Adaptability

There is a fine line between optimizing for delivery and over-optimizing for it. The pursuit of better delivery creates a narrow lens for viewing problems and acts as a filter for what kinds of decisions are allowed.

When delivery becomes the top priority, every challenge looks like a delivery problem, and options are judged by their impact on delivery. But over-optimizing for delivery makes the organization fragile and vulnerable to other risks:

- **Static Delivery Over Discovery:** A delivery focus suits a team well when they have an endless backlog of features to build. It doesn't help them develop the capabilities to research and decide _what_ to build, or to recognize that the market environment has shifted beneath their feet.
- **Process Over Progress:** A culture built around delivery makes it easier to implement highly structured, delivery-focused processes. However, those same practices will become that much more difficult to abandon when they stop being appropriate and start becoming a risk.
- **Dependency Over Autonomy:** Getting clear product direction from senior management creates an explicit mandate for teams. But relying exclusively on management to make product decisions ensures that teams remain slow, dependent, and incapable of developing their own decision-making capabilities.

## The Trade-Off Between Delivery and Adaptability

The value proposition of delivery optimization is clear, immediate, and far less disruptive to existing corporate structures than a true agile transformation. Organizations are consciously trading the slow, uncertain, and uncomfortable work of systemic change for the visible, easily measurable outputs they get from focusing on delivery.

While this is a legitimate business choice, it shouldn't be confused with agility or organizational adaptability. A delivery focus explicitly prioritizes output, and makes trade-offs with the principles that support flexibility—such as team autonomy, team purpose, optimizing cognitive load, and more.

## Building the Capacity to Evolve

The ultimate risk of "Agile delivery" is systemic calcification. By building top-down management structures designed to improve delivery, organizations over-optimize for a single way of working, actively destroying their own ability to pivot.

Organizational resilience cannot be found in a product backlog, nor can it be engineered through standardized models. It must be cultivated by reducing individual cognitive load, fostering multi-disciplinary capabilities, and giving teams the safety to pull new ways of working.
