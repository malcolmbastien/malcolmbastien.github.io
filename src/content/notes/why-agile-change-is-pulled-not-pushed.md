When companies are undergoing an Agile transformation, the decision on how to approach practice changes, either "push" or "pull" based change, will lead them to very different outcomes. Even when the practices are the same, these two approaches produce different impacts on people, teams, and the overall system of work.

**Push-based change** occurs when a practice or framework is mandated downward, either by a centralized group, a team's manager, or external consultants. This approach works like a structured process. It relies on a sequence of steps: selection, approval, change management, communication, training, and reporting.

**Pull-based change**, by contrast, is more of an organizational capability. It's a type of self-organization where a team identifies a challenge and pulls in a practice or a new way of working to solve it. Instead of a series of activities, it requires certain capabilities to exist: Teams need to have agency, the autonomy to self-organize, the empowerment to adjust processes without permission, the ability to adapt internal roles and responsibilities, the ability to diagnose challenges and co-create solutions, and the data systems to provide them with metrics to measure their progress.

### Problems Come from Push

When management or consultants push a practice onto a team, they bypass the team's own problem-solving process. With push-based change, the team is reduced to the _object_ of the change, while management acts as the _subject_, the ones making the decision.

When companies pursue push-based change, "practice compliance" always becomes some part of the system. If results do not improve, the discussion can start to focus on the level of practice compliance rather than on the appropriateness of the approach. When process adherence becomes a goal, it takes focus away from measuring the value were intended deliver.

> [!NOTE]
> With push-based change, the tool becomes the goal.

Pull-based change avoids this goal-seeking behaviour. Because pull-based change relies on empowered, self-organizing teams, it eliminates the need for tracking and reduces the surface area for managers to micro-manage teams.

Instead of monitoring whether a team is following the process they themselves came up with, pull-based change creates more focus on how successful a team is at achieving its desired outcomes.

### Pulling the Next Best Step

In an Agile context, teams should not be judged on their ability to resolve complex problems perfectly on the first try. Embarking down the path of pull-based change doesn't just mean giving teams the responsibility to solve a problem, it also requires building a continuous improvement culture where teams can:

1. Identify a challenge.
2. Formulate a working theory.
3. Run a small experiment.
4. Gather feedback.
5. Learn and adjust their approach.

Compared with push-based changes focused on implementing solutions, pull-based change is better aligned with Agile principles. Rather than trying to get it right the first time, pull is a process of incremental, iterative adjustments that gives teams more ownership, more motivation and builds trust.

### Pulling Change as a System Capability

Unlike push-based change, which is typically a management decision, pull-based change can't just happen. Pulling change is not a decision; it is a capability of a healthy, supportive organizational system.

Pull requires the right organizational support and environment to succeed:

- **Managerial Trust:** Managers shift from controlling processes to setting boundaries, and trust their teams with the autonomy to adjust their own practices.
- **Psychological Safety:** Teams have the safety for members to openly discuss their challenges, bottlenecks, expose knowledge gaps, and propose new ways of working.
- **Internal Role Plasticity:** Teams can flexibly adjust internal roles and responsibilities to accommodate new ways of working.
- **Clear Goals and Information:** Teams are aligned with their management on their goals and outcomes. Teams have the information they need to understand their current situation and to track the impact of their changes.

Another requirement for organizations that want to support pull-based change is to create a safe-to-fail culture. If a team's change fails to deliver the expected results, the team needs to know that management won't come in and take over.

### Conclusion

> "True agility means that teams are constantly working to evolve their processes to deal with the particular obstacles they are facing at any given time."
> —Lean Enterprise

Both approaches to change have their own challenges.

With **push-based change**, the challenges include selecting the right process, designing new systems, change management, rollout, communication, training, and reporting.

With **pull-based change**, the challenges include developing the organizational system that enables pull-based change: increasing team and management maturity, building trust between management and teams, creating a continuous improvement culture, fostering psychological safety and removing micromanagement
