As large organizations roll out AI tools, over eagerness to adopt new technology needs to be balanced against the risk of using AI to do the wrong things better.

Instead of improving underlying conditions, AI can make it dangerously easy to only focus on building solutions to treat symptoms in ways that weren't possible before.

## Easier Symptoms, Harder Causes

Imagine a senior manager who's always busy in back-to-back meetings. They are constantly context-switching, preparing status reports, and putting out fires. They are the bottleneck, causing teams to get stuck waiting on them for decisions.

A band-aid response would be to treat this as a time-management problem: _"Let's use AI to help this manager draft emails, write reports, and build presentations faster."_

This looks like a win because it relieves the manager's immediate pain. But because AI has made dealing with the symptoms so much easier to handle, nobody stops to ask why the manager was so overloaded in the first place. By making the symptoms cheaper to manage, AI pushes the hidden, more complex root causes even further out of reach.

## Lowering the Cost of Bad Ideas

Large organizations naturally prefer additive solutions—adding new processes like layers of an onion rather than peeling the old ones away. Because AI brings the cost of generating outputs down to virtually nothing, it is now feasible to throw an AI solution at organizational annoyances whenever they surface.

It is now incredibly easy to create solutions that cover up root causes. AI will gladly do what you tell it. It will generate slides to tell any story an organization wants to hear, but it cannot fix a broken decision-making process unless prompted.

At some point, someone needs to step back and ask: _"Why are we producing thousands of AI-generated slide decks in the first place?"_

To ensure AI is actually helping, organizations need to look at the what's really going on and ask three questions:

**What is the real challenge?**

In order to create solutions that have a meaningful impact, you need to look past surface-level issues and ask why things are working the way they are in the first place. Take the time to identify the root causes and bottlenecks.

**What kind of value is this driving?**

What happens after the AI solutions are implemented? Do they add value to the core product and value proposition, or do they just help people better manage non-value-adding work?

**Should AI be solving this at all?**

AI is effective at creating band-aids that make broken systems tolerable. But using AI to ease a symptom is waste if it causes you to avoid hard human work of fixing a broken process.

## Start with Outcomes

When anyone can use AI to create anything instantly, the real differentiator is what happens in people's heads. Success will depend on situational awareness—how people see problems, identify challenges, and choose what _not_ to do.

We shouldn't reward people just for using AI. We should reward them for tackling the right challenges.

Left unchecked, AI will easily supercharge existing top-down, command-and-control power structures and wasteful practices. Because creating output is virtually free, organizational waste is cheaper and easier to manufacture than ever before.

You can do almost anything thanks to AI, but should you?

The value of a solution is always in its outcomes, not the volume of its inputs.
