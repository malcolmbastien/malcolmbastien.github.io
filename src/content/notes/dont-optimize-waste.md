# Communication and Lean Waste

Agile coaches and change agents often get pulled toward optimizing the existing ways things work in organizations. Organizations have a hard time doing away with what already exists, so change goals are often targeted at optimizing existing processes. People frequently want guidance on how to make their status meetings more effective, or improve the performance of existing team structures.

But tweaking existing processes isn't an effective strategy for making significant improvements. Real improvement, requires starting with the right goals, and doing that requires stepping back from the noise of day-to-day work, and looking past what people are doing or what they're telling you. It requires being objective and looking at things differently.

One way to look at work differently is through the lens of lean manufacturing and waste.

## What Kind of Communication?

In large corporations, "communication" has a lot of interpretations. When people talk about communication problems, it can be helpful to identify what kind of communication is actually happening.

- Is it updates to management?
- Is it coordination between teams?
- Is it giving teams instructions?
- Is it getting approvals or sign-offs?

Once you know what kind of communication is happening, you can have to figure out _why_ it's happening. It's dangerous to try to solve a system problem by only going off of one person's perspective, you need a fuller picture before you can can start considering interventions.

## Three Categories of Waste

To escape the trap of optimizing symptoms, start looking at communication activities through one of these three types of waste.

### Failure Demand (Rework)

Work created by earlier failures, costing capacity that could have been used for something else instead. Failure demand can come from poor quality, defects, shifting priorities, or miscommunication.

> **Communication Examples of Failure Demand:**
> - Clarification meetings to update requirements.
> - Incident calls to problem-solve production issues.
> - Meetings to redo project plans.

Address failure demand through prevention. Trace it back to its source and determining how it could have been avoided. Consider solutions that build quality into the process.

### Necessary Non-Value-Adding Work

Necessary Non-Value-Adding Work is non-value adding for the customer, but is still required. The work is inherently wasteful, but you have to do it, so the strategy is to optimize it.

> **Communication Examples of Necessary Non-Value-Adding Work:**
> - Release notes and communicating deployment plans.
> - Cross-team coordination meetings.
> - Quarterly planning for team alignment and dependencies

The work needs to get done, but the longer it takes the more waste is created. To handle Necessary Non-Value-Adding Work, shrink batch sizes, tighten feedback loops and improve lead times. Your goal is to get back to the value-adding work as quickly as possible.

### Pure Waste

Pure waste is work that adds zero value to the product or the end user. It makes things take longer and cost more money.

> **Communication Examples of Pure Waste:**
> - Management status reports
> - Approval and sign-off gates.
> - "Just-in-case" updates between teams

There's always a reason why pure waste exists, but corporate explanations tend to hide the real reasons (such as organizational fear, low trust, or risk avoidance). Your job is to eliminate these activities.

Investigate the real purpose of these activities, and address the root causes. Avoid falling into the trap of optimizing pure waste activities. Do not make prettier status reports or get alignment on useless workflows—find ways to remove them entirely.

## Reduce the Need for Communication

Instead of enhancing the current system by improving communicating, redesign the system to improve flow:

- **Shift Authority:** Give teams the local authority and tools to take action themselves.
- **Shrink Batch Sizes:** Reduce batch sizes to lower coordination complexity.
- **Build Information Radiators:** Create information systems so stakeholders can self-serve the data they need.
- **Restructure Teams:** Build cross-functional, stream-aligned teams with end-to-end ownership. Keep communication local, and reduce the need for top-down or inter-team coordination.

## Conclusion

Avoid taking stated problems as face value. Look past the day-to-day noise of what people are doing and saying. Real, high-leverage change requires systems thinking and a focus on end-to-end value streams. Look out for sources of waste and avoid locally optimizing practices that should instead be either prevented or eliminated.
