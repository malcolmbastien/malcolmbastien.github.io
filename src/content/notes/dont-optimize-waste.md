# The Agile Coach's Trap: Stop Optimizing Waste. Fix the System.

When working as an Agile coach or change agent there's always a force of gravity that organizations have, which tries to pull you into optimizing existing ways of working. Because of this force, goals are often designed around optimizing existing processes and routines. They will ask you how they can make their status meetings more effective, bring processes under control, or improve the performance of existing team structures.

But focusing on optimizing existing processes is not an effective way to change a system. It creates a lot of activity and effort, but only to produces marginal results. In order create significant improvement, you have to work towards the right goals, and to do that requires removing yourself from the noise of day-to-day work, looking past what people are doing and even what they're telling you. It requires being objective and looking at things through the different lenses. One of these lenses is the lens of lean manufacturing and waste.

## Three Categories of Waste

To escape the trap of optimizing symptoms, start noticing when existing activities fit into one of the three well-known types of waste.

### Failure Demand (Rework)

Failure demand is waste that creates _additional_ work.

Failure demand creates additional work, costing capacity that could have been used for something else instead. Failure demand typically happens when teams suffer from poor quality, defects, changing priorities, or miscommunications causing additional work. 

Addressing failure demand involves tracing it back to its source, and determining how it could have been avoided. Create the conditions that build quality into the process so teams can do the right thing, or at least avoid doing the wrong thing.

### Necessary Non-Value-Adding Work

Work that is non-value adding but still necessary for customers is called "Necessary Non-Value-Adding Work" or Type 1 Muda. A defining feature here is that the work *is* wasteful, but you *have* to do it, so the strategy is optimize it.

(Examples and reasons for Necessary Non-Value-Adding Work)

Shrink batch sizes and tighten feedback loops. Your goal is to compress the time it takes to get data, minimizing the windows of silence where value rots.

### Pure Waste

Pure waste, or Type 2 Muda, is work that adds zero value to the end product. Its practical effect is making things take longer and more expensive with nothing to show for it.

As tempting as it may be, avoid falling into the trap of optimizing pure waste activities. Find ways to remove them.

There's always a reason why pure waste exists. These reasons are sometime hidden and non-obvious. Your job is to investigate _why_ the company relies on these activities, address those root causes, and help them find and adopt better alternatives.

## Why Communicate at All?

In large corporations, "communication" is a tricky word. It’s a label used to describe many different things. In order to improve flow, part of the work is to listen for when people talk about "communication" and identify what kind of communication is actually happening.

- Is it updates to management?
- Is it coordination between teams?
- Is it giving teams instructions?
- Is it getting approvals or sign-offs?

The phrase "This meeting could have been an email." is highly relevant to the activity of identifying waste.

Along with what *kind* of communication is happening, you also have to figure out *why* it's happening. Once you understand the what and the why, you can start figuring out alternatives.

### Comparing Workflows

- **The Old Workflow (Push):** Work is pushed down by a static project plan, forcing system state to remain hidden in functional silos. This lack of transparency requires continuous, manual narration to stakeholders, creating a heavy and expensive "communication tax."
- **The New Workflow (Pull):** Work is actively pulled by actual team capacity, making the true state of delivery fully visible inside the pipeline. The system effectively speaks for itself, reducing operational communication to rare, casual check-ins.

Hierarchical reporting structures create an artificial demand for upward information flow. Each management layer adds an administrative "communication tax" to protect itself, sparking an organizational arms race of slide decks and steering committees that remove zero actual risk.

Instead of enhancing the current system by improving communicating, think about ways to redesign the value stream to improve flow.

- **Shift Authority:** Stop teams from pushing data up to managers for decisions. Give teams the local authority and tools to take action themselves.
- **Shrink Batches:** Reduce batch sizes to automatically lower overall coordination complexity.
- **Build Information Radiators:** Create live information systems so stakeholders can self-serve the data they need, exactly when they need it.
- **Restructure Teams:** Build cross-functional, stream-aligned teams with end-to-end ownership. Keeping communication local and intra-team drastically cuts down the need for top-down, inter-team alignment.

When you shift an organization toward stream-aligned teams that own a feature from initial idea to production, you reduce the need for inter-team coordination.

## The Solution: Pull Systems and Observability

By focusing on the bigger picture, you can better help teams avoid optimizing their symptoms.

As a coach, your goal shouldn't be to optimize existing systems, instead it should be about helping organizations make significant performance improvements.

Keeping a sharp perspective on what's happening, and being able to relate that to the lean types of waste can help you from locally optimizing existing practices.
