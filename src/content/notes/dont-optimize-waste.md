# Communication and Lean Waste

When working as an Agile coach or change agent, organizations always exert a type of gravity that tries to pull you into optimizing existing ways of working. Because of this force, change goals are often targeted at optimizing existing processes and structures. People will ask you how they can make their status meetings more effective, or improve the performance of existing team structures.

But optimizing existing processes is not an effective strategy to making significant improvements. It creates a lot of activity and effort, but only produces marginal results. In order to create significant improvement, you have to work towards the right goals, and doing that requires removing yourself from the noise of day-to-day work, looking past what people are doing and even what they're telling you. It requires being objective and looking at things differently.

One way to look at work differently is through the lens of lean manufacturing and waste.

## What Kind of Communication?

In large corporations, "communication" is a tricky word. It’s a label used to describe many different things. In order to improve flow, part of the work is to listen for when people talk about "communication" and identify what kind of communication is actually happening.

- Is it updates to management?
- Is it coordination between teams?
- Is it giving teams instructions?
- Is it getting approvals or sign-offs?

Along with what kind of communication is happening, you also have to figure out _why_ it's happening. Once you understand the what and the why, you can start figuring out alternatives.

> [!NOTE]
> The popular saying  _"This meeting could have been an email"_ captures this idea of how communication, when done poorly, creates waste.

## Three Categories of Waste

To escape the trap of optimizing symptoms, start noticing when existing activities fit into one of the three well-known types of waste.

### Failure Demand (Rework)

Failure demand is waste that creates additional work, costing capacity that could have been used for something else instead. Failure demand typically happens when teams suffer from poor quality, defects, changing priorities, or miscommunications.

> **Communication Examples of Failure Demand:**
> - Clarification meetings update requirements.
> - Incident calls to problem-solve a production issues.
> - Meetings to redo project plans.

Addressing failure demand involves prevention and tracing it back to its source and determining how it could have been avoided. Create the conditions that build quality into the process so teams can do the right thing, or at least avoid doing the wrong thing.

### Necessary Non-Value-Adding Work

Work that is non-value adding but still necessary for customers or compliance reasons is called "Necessary Non-Value-Adding Work". A defining feature here is that the work is inherently wasteful, but you have to do it, so the strategy is to optimize it.

> **Communication Examples of Necessary Non-Value-Adding Work:**
> - Preparing release notes or communicating deployment plans.
> - Coordination meetings between different teams.
> - Quarterly or annual planning meetings to align teams, dependencies and resources.

The work needs to get done, but the longer it takes the more waste is created. To handle Necessary Non-Value-Adding Work, shrink batch sizes, tighten feedback loops and improve lead times. Your goal is to compress the time it takes so you can get back to focusing on the value-adding work as quickly as possible.

### Pure Waste

Pure waste is work that adds zero value to the product or the end user. It makes things take longer and cost more money.

> **Communication Examples of Pure Waste:**
> - Management status reports
> - Approval and sign-off gates.
> - "Just-in-case" updates between teams

There's always a reason why pure waste exists, but corporate explanations tend to hide the real reasons (such as organizational fear, low trust, or risk avoidence). Your job is to eliminate these activities. Investigate why the read purpose of these activities, address the root causes, and help the company satisfy their needs without relying on wasteful activities.

Avoid falling into the trap of optimizing pure waste activities. Do not make a prettier status report or get alignment on the importance of a useless workflow—find ways to remove them entirely.

## Reduce the Need for Communication

Instead of enhancing the current system by improving communicating, think about ways to redesign the value stream to improve flow:

- **Shift Authority:** Stop teams from pushing data up to managers for decisions. Give teams the local authority and tools to take action themselves.
- **Shrink Batch Sizes:** Reduce batch sizes to lower overall coordination complexity.
- **Build Information Radiators:** Create information systems so stakeholders can self-serve the data they need, when they need it.
- **Restructure Teams:** Build cross-functional, stream-aligned teams with end-to-end ownership. Keep communication local and intra-team and reduce the need for top-down or inter-team coordination.

## Conclusion

High-leverage change requires systems thinking and a focus on end-to-end value streams. Look out for sources of waste and avoid locally optimizing practices that should instead be either prevented or eliminated.

To create significant improvements, look past the day-to-day noise of what people are doing and saying. When teams complain about a lack of alignment or wanting to improve existing processes, take that as a hint of where to start your investigation.
