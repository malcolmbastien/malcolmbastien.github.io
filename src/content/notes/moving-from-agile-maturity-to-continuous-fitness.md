# Stop Auditing Practices, Start Solving Outcomes

Most Agile transformations start the same way: implement team-level practices, measure progress against a checklist. Because of the checklist thinking approach to agile practices, after a team implements them people just sort of move on. Some maturity models implemented a graded system ("level 1" vs "level 2" standups) but it's still ultimately about comparing what a team does to what's written on a list.

Starting with practices, filling up people's calendars, installing new team models just increases the organizational cost of agile, it makes it more disruptive, and people end up thinking agile's a joke of pointless roles and meetings.

For most of people doing agile, I don't think any agile maturity models have any value for organizations because what's important are not practices, but customer fitness. And since customer needs, team challenges and the environment keep changing, fitness has to be continuously evaluated.

## Why Maturity Checklists Don't Work

The standard way to measure an agile transformation is to build a rubric of practices and roles. Check off more practices, get a higher "agile" score.

That's the maturity model trap. A model scoring teams on ceremony presence measures compliance, not capability. Fitness for purpose comes from delivering value, cutting waste, and adapting to new information—none of which show up on a standard audit.

The more prominent these models get, the more they become the goal. When a proxy metric becomes the target, behavior shifts. We incentivize performing the practice over achieving the outcome. You end up with a cargo cult: rituals run with precision, business problems untouched.

Static rubrics also miss what actually matters:

- They can show a daily stand-up happens. They can't tell you if it resolves blockers or just functions as a status report.
- They don't ask whether a practice makes sense for a team's situation. A stable, low-dependency team gets forced into the same cadence as a high-volatility one.
- They can't tell the difference between practices a team discovered organically to fix a real friction point and practices a PMO dictated top-down.

When the environment changes, the rubric doesn't.

## Measuring fitness in context

Fitness isn't a static state. It's how well a system meets stakeholder needs across changing conditions. Measuring it means watching outcomes, not checking boxes.

Most transformations try to fix this at the ground level. They obsess over team ceremonies—stand-ups, backlog grooming, sprint planning. These are tied to execution, and organizations love visible, trackable busywork.

But team-level ceremonies can't evaluate themselves. Without higher-level feedback, teams get really good at running "the perfect process" while having no idea whether any of it matters to the business.

The Fit for Purpose framework changes the question. Not "are we doing Agile?" but "are we meeting customer needs?" You identify what matters to customers, then measure against that instead of a generic framework.

Kanban's higher-level cadences are how you check whether team practices are helping:

- Service Delivery Review: Are we delivering on commitments to customers, and are our workflows actually fit for that?
- Operations Review: Across teams, where's the friction? How's demand balancing against capacity?
- Risk Review: What's blocking value flow, and are we managing dependencies well?
- Strategy Review: Does our operational capability still match where we're trying to go?

Without these, team-level agility is just local optimization.

## How to Start Solving Outcomes

If you want to stop checking boxes, the transformation itself has to change. Not just for coaches—for leaders and teams who want their agile work to actually mean something.

If you don't start with higher-level reviews, how can you make an informed decision about which agile practices to implement? Without a baseline of how value currently flows, choosing team-level rituals is just guessing.

Instead of starting with team-level checklists, think outside-in.

1. **Install higher-level reviews first:** Set up the operational, risk, and strategy reviews from day one. Before changing anything, use them to see how work moves through the organization and is delivered to customers.
2. **Assess current fitness:** The reviews will surface where there's issues in service delivery: Lead time, quality, reliability, impact, customer satisfaction, business value.
3. **Understand improvement opportunities:** Now you investigate what's causing the problems. Instead of jumping to implementing team-level practices like stand-ups, practices start with a clear goal "Lead time is slow because nobody sees cross-team dependencies until it's too late."
4. **Implement organizational and team practices focused on impact:** Introduce practices only where they directly fix those problems. And keep using the high-level reviews to measure whether they're working.

The point is to create a connected feedback system. Customer outcomes drive practices, practices support high-level goals. The reviews connect team and organization practices to customer and outcome metrics.

Avoid setting down a path of following an agile maturity model, and believing the promise that "If you want to succeed and be agile, you need to do X." A maturity model is a static artifact and can't tell you if you're succeeding, you need the feedback mechanisms that bridge what your organization is doing, with what effect they're having on the outside world.
