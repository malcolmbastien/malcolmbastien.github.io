# The AI Coach Trap: Faster at Selling Answers You Haven't Earned

When encountering a complex problem at work, our default instinct is to rush toward a solution. I call this the tendency, especially of Agile Coaches, to lock onto the first viable idea that pops into their heads and immediately shift into execution mode, going with the "first, worst solution."

In environments where this happens, In the language of Daniel Kahneman, you have people running around building System 1 responses to systemic organizational problems.

These days, the danger is that generative AI makes it incredibly easy to develop these shallow ideas. AI can turn a half-thought-out hunch into a polished, authoritative presentation.

Thanks to AI, it's possible to be more productive than ever and develop of solutions that:
- **Fail to integrate the people in those systems:** It is easy to design rigid structures, but much harder to create environments where people can adapt and use their full capabilities.
- **Create a reinforcing bias** toward specific answers and ways of working that will be incredibly difficult to unlearn later.
- **Optimize for the wrong goals.**

All you need is a rough idea for AI to instantly package your initial assumptions into a compelling narrative with professional quality slides, all without having to do any of the difficult work of understanding the problem.

## The process is the work

This is why the value of an Agile Coach—or any strategic leader—isn't in preparing slide decks or providing answers. Giving teams pre-packaged solutions, as polished as they are, robs them of the opportunity to learn and grow.

One problem of leveraging AI to sell people on professional-looking solutions, is that it jumps to the solution without going through the work. Real progress lies in helping teams solve their own problems using approaches rooted in agile principles:
- Collaborative problem framing
- Building shared understanding
- Running small experiments
- Learning through feedback

The process is the work. If you use AI to build convincing, authoritative presentations to sell your initial ideas, you are skipping that work. If a team walks away with a shiny new solution but hasn't grown as a collective unit, learned something new, or developed their internal capabilities, it is a failure of leadership.

Real organizational changes requires change in both the process and the people involved.

# Conclusion

Generating code with AI is one thing—code can be tested, failed, and refactored in seconds. Using AI to change organizational structures and policies is a different story.

Changes to organization structures and policies alter power structures and the patterns of how people interact. While process and structure changes create a burst of activity in the short term, the long-term consequences of a bad policy change might not surface until later.

In the short term, coaches and managers who use AI to generate polished slides for bad ideas at high speed might look successful in organizations that reward busyness and activity, but the cost of those first-worst solutions will be felt sooner or later.

AI should be used to help teams succeed, but we must do our best to make sure they are succeeding at the right things. Use AI to multiply the effect of your thinking, not to replace it.
