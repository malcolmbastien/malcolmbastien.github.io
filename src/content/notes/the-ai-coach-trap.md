# The AI Coach Trap: Faster at Selling Answers You Haven't Earned

When a complex problem shows up at work, the default instinct is to find a solution. I call this tendency, to lock onto the first idea that pops into people's heads, going with the "first, worst solution."

In the language of Daniel Kahneman, you have people running around building System 1 responses to system 2 problems. Fast intuition applied to problems that need slow, deliberate thinking

## AI Makes Shallow Solutions Look Polished

Generative AI makes it easy to develop half-formed ideas and turn them into a polished, authoritative presentation.

AI makes it possible to be more productive than ever and develop of solutions that:
- **Ignore the people in those systems:** Rigid structures are easy to design, but it's much harder to create environments where people can adapt and use their full capabilities.
- **Create a reinforcing bias** toward specific approaches that become difficult to unlearn later.
- **Optimize for the wrong goals** without anyone noticing until it's too late.

A rough idea is all it takes for AI to package your initial assumptions into a compelling narrative with professional quality slides, all without having to do any of the difficult work of actually understanding the problem.

## The Process Is the Work

An Agile Coach's value isn't in preparing slide decks or providing answers. Handing teams beautiful pre-packaged solutions robs them of the chance to figure things out themselves, of the opportunity to learn and grow.

Using AI to sell professional-looking solutions skips the work of understanding the problem. Real progress happens when teams solve their own problems through, collaborative problem framing, building shared understanding, running small experiments, and learning through feedback.

If you use AI to build convincing, authoritative presentations that teams buy-in to, you are skipping that work. If a team walks away with a shiny new solution but hasn't grown, learned something new, or developed their capabilities, it is a failure of leadership.

Real organizational changes requires change in both the process and the people involved.

# Structural Changes Last

Using Generating AI to write code is one thing; code can be tested, fail, and refactored in seconds. Using AI to change organizational structures and policies is a different story.

When you change how teams are structured or how decisions get made, you're changing power dynamics and interaction patterns. Process and structure changes create a burst of activity in the short term, but long-term consequences only surface later.

The use of Generative AI will be rewarded by organizations who like people who look productive and that like visible activity, but the cost of those first-worst solutions will come out sooner or later.

Use AI to help teams succeed, but make sure they are succeeding at the right things. Use to multiply the effect of your thinking, not to replace it.
