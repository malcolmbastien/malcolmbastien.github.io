# The AI Coach Trap: Faster at Selling Answers You Haven't Earned

When encountering a complex problem at work, our default instinct is to rush toward a solution. We lock onto the "first, worst solution"—the first viable idea that pops into our heads—and immediately shift into execution mode.

In the language of Daniel Kahneman, we are running around building System 1 responses to systemic organizational problems.

The danger today is that generative AI makes it incredibly easy to commit to and develop these shallow ideas. In minutes, AI can turn a half-thought-out hypothesis into a polished, authoritative presentation. Because AI is fundamentally sycophantic, it will enthusiastically validate and dress up whatever assumptions you feed it.

As a result, we are seeing an influx of solutions that:
- **Exceed people's capacity** to understand their systemic impact.
- **Optimize for the wrong goals**
- **Create a reinforcing bias** toward specific answers and ways of working that will be difficult to unlearn later.a

With AI, all you need is a rough idea to instantly package your initial assumptions into a compelling narrative with professional quality slides, all while skipping the time necessary to actually understand the problem.

## The process is the work

Just as in software development, you must assume your first solution is wrong. We only learn by engaging with the problem and collecting feedback.

The value of Agile Coaching isn't preparing slide decks for facilitating meetings. The value is in helping clients solve their own problems using agile principles and values. Coaches do this in part through exploring problem framing, building shared understanding, and identifying root causes rather than treating surface-level symptoms.

Trying to just sell solutions to clients fails for a few reasons:

1. **You are not the client.** They are the ones who have to live with the consequences of the decision. Coaches never have the complete picture or the same domain knowledge the client has about their business and industry.
2. **Agile Coaches need coaching expertise.** That expertise includes knowledge Agile principles and practices, and using those tools to help clients build better solutions. The job of Agile Coaches is not to give solutions to clients. Giving solutions robs clients of the opportunity to learn and grow.
3. **Imposed solutions breed dogma.** When an organization adopts someone else's pre-packaged answer, the practices inevitably freeze into a rigid, unquestioned script. As reality drifts, the process becomes inappropriate, creating entirely new crises.

The process is the work. If you are using AI to build up convincing, authoritative presentations to sell your ideas, it's skipping the work. If a team walks away with a solution, but hasn't grown as a collective unit or developed new capabilities, it's really only a short-term solution. You've simply taken the cognitive load off the client, handed them an artifact, and prevented them from learning anything. You haven't coached anything.

# Conclusion

Generating code with AI is one thing—code can be tested, failed, and refactored in seconds. Using AI to rewrite organizational policies is a completely different story. These changes alter how human beings interact, and in slower organizations with long lead times, the real, damaging effects of a bad policy might not surface for months.

In the short term, coaches who use AI to generate polished slides for bad ideas at supersonic speeds will look highly successful. But the debt of those first-worst solutions will eventually come due.

AI should be used to help teams succeed at the right things—by letting them multiply the affect of their thinking, not replace it.
