When a complex problem shows up at work, the default instinct is to find a solution. I call this tendency to lock onto the first idea that pops into people's heads the "first, worst solution."

AI makes it easier to apply fast intuition to problems and removes the need for slow, deliberate thinking.

## AI Makes Shallow Solutions Look Polished

> [!NOTE]
> We should be designing solutions with people, not for people.

Generative AI makes it easy to turn half-formed ideas into polished, authoritative solutions.

AI makes it possible to be more productive than ever and develop solutions that:

- **Ignore the people in those systems:** Rigid structures are easy to design, but it's harder to create environments where people can adapt and use their full capabilities. 
- **Create a reinforcing bias** toward specific approaches that become difficult to unlearn later.
- **Optimize for the wrong goals**.

A rough idea is all it takes for AI to package your initial assumptions into a compelling narrative with professional-quality slides, all without having to do any of the difficult work of actually understanding the problem.

## The Process Is the Work

An Agile Coach's value isn't in preparing slide decks or giving teams answers. Handing teams beautiful pre-packaged solutions robs them of the opportunity to figure things out themselves and to learn and grow.

Using AI to pitch professional-looking solutions skips the work of working through the problem with people, of exploring possibilities. Real progress happens when teams solve their own problems through exploring different problem frames, building shared understanding, running small experiments, and learning through feedback.

Using AI to jump straight to the solution skips that work. Teams become recipients of solutions instead of designers. If a team is handed a new solution but hasn't grown, learned something new or developed their capabilities, it's a leadership failure.

Real organizational change requires change in both the process and the people involved.

# Structural Changes Last

Using Generative AI to write code is one thing; code can be tested, fail, and refactored in seconds. Using AI to change organizational structures and policies is a different story.

When you change how teams are structured and how decisions get made, you're changing  power dynamics and interaction patterns. Process and structural changes create a burst of activity in the short term, but long-term consequences surface only later.

Regardless of its results, the use of Generative AI will be rewarded by organizations that like their people to look busy and like seeing activity. The cost of those "first-worst" solutions shows up later in through the gaps trying that trying to design a solution in a complex environment inevitably misses. Resilient systems are emergent, are't designed.

Use AI to help teams multiply and scale their thinking, not to do the thinking for them.
