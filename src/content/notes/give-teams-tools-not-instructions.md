When trying to improve the performance of a complex system, it can be tempting to resort to instructions and micromanagement. Faced with uncertainty, dynamic conditions, and an evolving understanding of the problem, it's natural to want to create stability and control through clear goals, structures, and processes.

But trying to improve performance by treating knowledge work like a predictable, mechanical process limits what a team can achieve. If you want high-performing teams, stop giving instructions and start giving your team the tools to succeed.

## Instructions Make the Process More Important Than the Goal

> [!NOTE]
> You can prescribe actions, but you can't prescribe how people think or behave.

When organizations want to ensure quality, speed, or predictability, the common approach is to design processes declare explicit goals. They roll out standardized frameworks, roles, rituals, and workflows.

But boxing a team in with prescriptive instructions shifts their cognitive load. Instead of solving the problem, their primary goal becomes complying with the process. This shift always introduces unintended, systemic consequences:

- **Going Through the Motions:** Teams hold ceremonies they don’t understand or find valuable, turning dynamic problem-solving into compliance rituals.
- **Doing What They're Told:** People blindly follow practices they actively know don’t work because deviation is penalized, killing the team's ability to self-correct.
- **Box Checking:** Behaviours become focused on ticking boxes and satisfying internal bureaucracy rather than delivering value.
- **Losing Sight of the "Why":** People follow the letter of the law but drift away from the spirit of the goals the process was trying to achieve.
- **Staying Out of Trouble:** The team's ultimate goal shifts from achieving business impact to avoiding getting in trouble with management.

When you introduce a prescriptive process to solve a problem, that process doesn’t just guide the work, it becomes a part of the system. It introduces a new, implicit set of process-related goals related to things like tasks, roles, and process policies. Over time, the system inherently redirects its energy to optimize for these internal mechanics. This causes the emergent process goals to starve, distort, and actively interfere with the system's original objectives.

## Alignment Is Not the Same as Instruction

The alternative to micromanagement isn't giving teams complete autonomy to wander. The answer is aligned-autonomy.

Leaders shouldn't dictate the path; they should define the objective and ensure the team has the tools they need to succeed. When a team deeply understands the _what_ and the _why_, they are free to exercise responsible autonomy, peer self-regulation, initiative, and adaptability to solve the _how_.

## Tools to Give Teams

Management and the wider organization should act as an enabling platform for teams, helping them meet their goals. Organizational leadership should feel like a source of support, not like inspectors.

This means shifting from giving instructions to providing teams a toolkit that empowers local decision-making:

- **Target Outcomes:** Equip teams with clear, measurable business problems to solve rather than task backlogs. Define success by impact rather than output.
- **Visibility:** Provide total clarity into the end-to-end workflow and the impact of the team's work. Give them the visual tools to spot bottlenecks, balance capacity, and self-correct.
- **A Shared Language:** Create a clear definition of success using shared tools such as OKRs, SLAs, and lead-time metrics, so teams can objectively evaluate their performance.
- **The Authority to Experiment:** Grant permission to test new approaches, alter workflows, or adapt team dynamics locally.
- **Psychological Safety:** Ensure that management acts strictly as an enabler and coach, decentralizing traditional power structures so employees feel safe to point out issues and friction points.
- **Autonomous Decision-Making:** Define clear decision boundaries, budgets, and guidelines for local ownership. Teams can move faster when they know where their authority begins and ends.
- **Guardrails:** Instead of policing daily practices, specify operational boundaries that protect the system without controlling the team's work such as security baselines, compliance, or architecture standards.
- **Clear Roadblocks:** Focus leadership on removing system dependencies, organizational friction points, and cross-team blockers that teams cannot resolve on their own.

## Shifting to Outcome Ownership

To shift from giving instructions to giving tools, management needs to take a new stance.

Relying on giving teams instructions creates a brittle organization. Outcome ownership happens when you give teams the tools to succeed, which starts by setting up independent value streams. The goal is to set up a team structure that can own a "whole task" from start to finish.

When teams own their outcomes, they can focus on navigating real external constraints rather than wasting energy managing internal friction and bureaucracy.

The role of leadership is to ensure teams are set up for success. This includes having the right tools: the structure, people, autonomy, safety, information, and feedback systems to achieve their goals. Legal, financial, and performance guardrails should also be in place to protect the business.
