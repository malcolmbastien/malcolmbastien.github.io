When a complex system isn't performing up to people's expectations, it can be tempting to resort to tighten control through more instructions and micromanagement. When faced with the uncertainty of dynamic conditions, and an evolving understanding of the problem, it's natural to want to create stability and control through clear goals, defined structures, and explicit processes.

But treating knowledge work like a predictable, mechanical process limits what a team can achieve. High-performing teams don't need more instructions, they need better tools.

## Instructions Hijack the Goal

> [!NOTE]
> You can prescribe actions, but you can't prescribe how people think or behave.

Organizations that want better quality, speed, or predictability, typically design solutions around explicit goals. They roll out standardized frameworks, roles, rituals, and workflows.

But prescriptive instructions affects not just what teams do, but also how. Instead of solving the actual problem, teams start focusing more on complying with the process. This shift introduces unintended consequences:

- **Going Through the Motions:** Teams hold ceremonies they don’t understand or find valuable.
- **Doing What They're Told:** People blindly follow practices they actively know don’t work because because pushing back gets punished.
- **Box Checking:** Behaviours become focused on ticking boxes rather than delivering value.
- **Losing Sight of the "Why":** People follow the letter of the process but lose sight of what the process was trying to achieve.
- **Staying Out of Trouble:** The team's primary goal shifts to survival; from achieving impact to avoiding getting in trouble.

A process doesn't just guide how a system works, it becomes part of the system. It creates new, implicit goals related to things like task, role, and process compliance. Over time, the new process starves, distorts, and replaces the system's original objectives.

## Alignment Isn't the Same as Instruction

The alternative to micromanagement isn't giving teams complete autonomy to wander. The answer is a clear purpose, a target, and the autonomy to reach it.

Leaders shouldn't dictate the path; they should define the objective and ensure the team has the tools they need to succeed.

When a team deeply understands the _what_ and the _why_, they are free to exercise responsible autonomy, peer self-regulation, initiative, and adaptability to solve the _how_.

## Tools to Give Teams

Management and the organization should act as an enabling platform for teams. Organizational leadership should feel like a support system, not like inspectors.

This means shifting from giving instructions to providing teams the tools to succeed:

- **Target Outcomes:** Give teams a clear business problem to solve rather than a backlog. Define success by impact, not output.
- **Visibility:** Give teams the ability to see the end-to-end workflow, spot bottlenecks, balance capacity, and self-correct.
- **A Shared Language:** Create a clear definition of success and the ability to evaluate their performance using tools like OKRs, SLAs, and lead-time metrics.
- **The Authority to Experiment:** Let teams test new approaches and to modify their own workflows.
- **Psychological Safety:**  Make management an enabler and coach. Decentralize power so employees feel safe to point out issues and friction points.
- **Autonomous Decision-Making:** Define clear decision boundaries, budgets, and guidelines for ownership. Teams can move faster when they know where their authority begins and ends.
- **Guardrails:** Set boundaries that protect the organization without micromanaging the team's work.
- **Clear Roadblocks:** Focus leadership on removing system dependencies, organizational friction points, and cross-team blockers that teams can't handle alone.

## Shifting to Outcome Ownership

Instruction-heavy organizations are brittle to change. When teams own their outcomes, they can focus on navigating real constraints rather than on fighting internal friction and bureaucracy.

The role of leadership is to ensure teams are set up for success. This includes having the right tools: the structure, people, autonomy, safety, information, and feedback systems to achieve their goals.
