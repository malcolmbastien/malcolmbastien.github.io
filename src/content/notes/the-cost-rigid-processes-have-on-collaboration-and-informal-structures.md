Earlier this year, on a Reddit thread discussing mass layoffs at a major Canadian bank, a comment caught my attention:

_"When I first started, there was a lot of collaboration and informal networks. You got things done by helping folks out, who would then help you out when you needed it... Porter gets in there and calls in the McKinsey consultants, who tell him that the bank needs to kill this collaborative vibe, in favour of internal competition and a more commercial approach."_

The comment resonated with me because this pattern is one I see played out over and over at large companies as Agile continues its slow death and transformation into a process-heavy, management consultant-designed, efficiency-driven way of working.

Truly agile ways of working, ones that don't fit the corporate playbook, are rejected, and instead, we see them being replaced more and more with corporate agile operating models focused on explicit roles, team structures, processes, and metrics. They are sold by management consultants, bought by executives, and implemented by Agile Coaches, all of whom share the common trait of trying to remove variability and uncertainty from the system.

Underlying this transition is the core assumption that _more structured, ordered systems are inherently more effective._

Unfortunately, the processes designed to make an organization more "efficient" are the same ones that reduce agility, making it slow, rigid, and more fragile.

### Informal Structures and "Adaptive Capacity"

The book OpenSpace Beta describes three distinct, co-existing structures inside every company that explain why forcing internal order and efficiency on a company fails to create results:

1. **Formal Structure:** Positional power, hierarchy, formal authority, organizational structure, and control through compliance.
2. **Informal Structure:** The domain of human relationships, social and relationship power, influence, and trust.
3. **Value Creation Structure:** Where value creation happens. Reputation and mastery.

For an organization to satisfy its customers and survive changes in its environment, it's not enough to rely solely on its formal structure. Meaningful outcomes require the collaboration and coordination that largely depend on the informal and value-creation structures.

These structures enable a system's adaptive capacity. Adaptive capacity is the system's and people's potential and readiness to change how they work and adapt to changing situations, anomalies, and surprises. Tightly formalized roles, processes and ways of coordinating that prevent informal collaboration and eliminate value-creation networks, restricting the use of adaptive capacity.

It is an organization's people and their ability to self-organize, collaborate and adapt across the lines of the org chart and official processes that keep the system running. This unstructured, human "gap-bridging" is what makes organizations resilient. Yet management is not trained to recognize the Informal and Value Creation structures or the value they create. More often, these organic, emergent systems are seen as out-of-control variations from intended and desired, structured ways of working.

### The Preconditions for Action: Trust and Shared Experience

When managers eliminate informal networks to optimize for formal structures, they design systems based only on what they can see and measure. They ignore the intangible factors that drive real team effectiveness: trust, initiative, and relationships. While these elements cannot be designed, controlled, or measured, they enable high performance by enabling teams to bypass the friction of explicit methods through mutual trust and shared context.

Instead of relying on heavy documentation and constant alignment checks, individuals in high-trust teams can anticipate each other's needs and actions. They develop a shared way of looking at problems that allows them to encourage individual initiative while simultaneously achieving shared goals.

A formal structure or a framework cannot mandate this shared way of working. It is developed organically and slowly over time by teams working together, solving real problems, and sharing experiences. This dynamic coordination is what gives teams the speed and flexibility to handle complex environments—capabilities that formal processes cannot prescribe.

### A Better Way to Manage: Cultivation Over Control

Managers don't need to install standardized agile operating models to help their teams become more productive. In fact, doing so would more likely limit their ability to coordinate and adapt. Instead, management's goals should shift from installing systems to enabling the organizational factors that support informal structures and practices that support value creation.

The key is to create the right organizational environment where desirable, value-creating behaviours can emerge. These capabilities include:

- **Responsible-Autonomy:** Instead of imposing standardized workflows and templates, leaders give individual teams the authority to design and adapt their own processes.
- **Shared Context:** Rather than relying on written documentation and formal alignment checkpoints, leaders should focus on building trust and shared purpose and awareness. Teams build mutual trust and familiarity by working together through real-world problems, allowing them to anticipate and act cohesively without waiting for formal instructions.
- **Psychological Safety:** To enable agility, teams must have the safety to run experiments, learn from what doesn't work, and pivot quickly. Environments where the boss or the formal process prevents teams from acting will forever be rate-limited.

Avoid trying to design or "fix" behaviours explicitly by adding more processes or a new framework. Create an environment where teams are given the autonomy and encouragement to get a bit messy, collaborate, and let new ways of working emerge.
