A team can be following every practice in the book and still fail to adapt. A team can have almost no prescribed practices and be deeply effective. The difference isn't discipline or talent. It's what the team pays attention to — and that's shaped by the constraints they work under and the trust the organization extends to them.

When a team's environment is full of standards, their orientation shifts. They start to focus on the standards they are expected to follow. "Are we doing the stand-up correctly?" "Is the sprint commitment met?" "Is the burndown on track?" The standards become the work. The team is oriented toward compliance with the framework, and the framework becomes the primary thing they pay attention to.

When a team's environment is more fluid — when practices are changeable, when decisions are made locally — something different happens. The team has to pay attention to each other and to the environment. *What does this situation actually require?* *How is the team feeling this week?* *Is the way we're working still serving us?* The team's orientation shifts from following standards to sensing situations.

The two orientations are different in kind. A standards-focused team gets better at following standards. A sensing-focused team gets better at sensing. Each becomes more of what it practices.

---

## Where the Orientations Come From

Constraints shape where a team's attention goes. A team whose constraints sit mostly in the high-cost, long-time quadrant is being shaped toward the standards-focused orientation. A team whose constraints sit mostly in the low-cost, short-time quadrant is being shaped toward the sensing-focused orientation. The grid determines which one develops.

But the constraints don't tell the whole story. The orientations also reflect the trust the organization extends to the team.

The standards-focused orientation is shaped in low-trust environments. When an organization doesn't trust its teams to make good decisions, it standardizes those decisions and creates a framework the team can follow. The team learns to pay attention to the framework rather than to the situation. The "we do Scrum" framework gives managers something to point to when asking whether the team is doing the right thing. Standards are, in part, a substitute for trust.

The sensing-focused orientation is shaped in high-trust environments. When an organization trusts its teams to make good decisions, it leaves the decisions to the team. The team develops the muscle of paying attention to context, to each other, to what the situation actually requires.

Trust and orientation aren't emergent properties that drift over time. They're design choices. A system is built as high-trust or low-trust from the start, through the standards it sets, the authority it grants, and the space it leaves for teams to figure things out. The choice between the two is foundational.

---

## The Original Agile Values Were Pointing at This

The original signatories of the agile manifesto were pushing back against an environment where standards and processes had become the primary thing teams paid attention to. They wanted teams to be sensitive to their context, their customers, and each other. They saw the standards-focused orientation as a failure mode — one that produced predictability but not agility.

The standards-focused orientation has its place. In regulated industries, in safety-critical systems, in large organizations where coordination is the dominant challenge, paying attention to standards is exactly the right thing to do. But in environments where the situation changes faster than the standards can keep up, the sensing-focused orientation is the one that keeps the team effective.

The choice between the two orientations is foundational. Set it deliberately.
